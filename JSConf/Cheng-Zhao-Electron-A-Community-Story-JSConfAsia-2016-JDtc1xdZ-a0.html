Cheng Zhao: Electron - A Community Story - JSConf.Asia 2016

2016-12-18
Hello Everyone Today, I'm going to be talking about Electron Not the technical part. I will talk about how I built the community for electron and how the community of Electron helped it grow My name is Cheng And I created the Electron framework I'm currently working at Github and still working on Electron So if you have ever created an issue at Electron the repo you might known me because I'm usually the guy who closed your issues So before talking about Electron I need to talk about its history first It's still a relatively little project But the history originated from node-webkit And if you know about nw.js node-webkit is the older name of nw.js So the first edition of node-webkit is the node.js module It was not production-ready And then a year later I joined the development of node-webkit From that time node-webkit became a framework that you can develop desktop apps based on it It was basically the nw.js you know now And then a year later I started a new project named atom-shell And it was open-sourced and then renamed to Electron So the story of Electron started from node-webkit I joined the development of node-webkit when I was doing an internship at Intel At that time, node-webkit was a perfect project for interns Because it was interesting It was open-source And most importantly no one was using node-webkit So it doesn't matter if an intern messed up with it So I started as the only developer of node-webkit And because there were no users, I could just do whatever I want to the project And most importantly no one gave a shit about it so... My manager didn't care and, well, the company didn't care It was just a project for interns So for me, at that time the most important thing was to define what node-webkit should be So at that time I decided that I can probably rewrite webkit as the platform to develop desktop apps with html and node.js There were already many projects doing similar things, but... apparently all of them were shit So by working on node-webkit I can probably create a new generation project After spending a few months developing node-webkit Well, I finally got something that really worked My manager was happy with it because I was not wasting my time doing unrelated things It was a nice result for interns But a problem for me was that it still had no users I was just spending a few months entertaining myself entertaining my manager It was not real open-source So I decided to build a community of node-webkit and had everyone using it So the first thing I did was to build a small community at that time it was quite a simple one I used GitHub issues for bug reports questions and any type of thing And there was a mailing list on Google Groups There were people who were preferring the older type of discussion So after building the community I started to promote node-webkit and that's the most important thing for new projects, you need to make people know your project So at that time node-webkit didn't have a strong background even though it was a project sponsored by Intel but there was a only an intern working on it and the project was not under Intel's GitHub account It was actually under the account of an Intel employee who wrote the first edition of node-webkit When people saw node-webkit they were just seeing it's a simple project developed by no none So I needed to advertise it The thing I did was announcements at different mailing lists saying that I created a new awesome project Just come and use it some people might be interested in it they might ask very stupid questions Well, at that time no matter how stupid the questions might be I still needed to answer every one of them so I probably might have my first user And another important thing was I needed to keep publishing a new release So if I just do one release of the project and then do nothing later people would think this was just a hobby project If use it I will be fucked up in the end So the most important thing is to keep releasing new versions of the project After I kept doing so I finally got my first user That one was actually quite famous If you knew Light Table was an editor based on real web technology It was like the fist program project on Kick Starter So it gained a lot of It was a very famous project and it helped node-webkit get lots of attention So once we had a first user we might have a second and then more After that the most important thing became we needed to keep our first user How do you keep them? There's only one thing to do is just answer their questions and implement the features they want and fix the bugs they are troubled with They are getting support from you and they will be confident in your project So more and more people were using node-webkit and I was doing a good job of keeping them Somehow, it started to become famous So usually when your project becomes famous you actually have lots of choices You can ask for sponsorship from the big companies who use your project so you can get money by working on your own project Or you can ask some of the contributors of the project to get into your team so you have more people working on the project or you can even start a company or career based on your own project probably you can make huge money from it One of them was just to keep your project growing bigger and bigger As for me, it was a bit different Ater node-webkit became famous I did not work on it anymore Because if you still remember I did not own node-webkit I was just an intern that was doing an internship on the project Once node-webkit became famous as an intern, I just lost the leadership of the project It basically means I lost the control of the project It was not my project anymore I ended my internship and started the atom-shell project atom-shell, if you still remember, was the old name of Electron For the first year of Electron it was unlike the first year of node-webkit because this time the development of Electron was sponsored by GitHub because they were secretly developing the Atom editor they thought that node-webkit was a great platform, but it was not good enough so they hired me to work on the new atom-shell platform At that time, I was still the only developer for Electron That was fine because my only user was Atom editor and all I had to do was to make the Atom editor a great product based on atom-shell After working on Electron for one year it was open-sourced together with Atom and unlike the launch of node-webkit the launch of Electron was quite easy because it was a product of GitHub and there was an open-source project using Electron the first days of Electron we had we had lots of stars on GitHub and we got a lot of new users just within a few weeks Since we were not worried about getting new users the task for me became I needed to keep Electron a good project a project that people would be confident to use and a project that could be used for a very long time To achieve that, I just needed to keep the project well-maintained For the community, this time Slack was quite popular at that time It's much better than the older IRC channel It's very modern so we used Slack as our community channel we also added some more forums for discussion And this time, for Electron I didn't use mailing lists anymore because it was very hard to use and for GitHub issues, I wouldn't allow questions for when I developed node-webkit But that is generally bad when you project became quite big because there would be many people asking questions in the issues and it would distract the development of the project And to... make the project well-maintained one of the important things is to manage the issues well Basically, I think the central idea is to answer every issue you don't have to, like... do support for every people, but for issues you need to make people know that this issue has been looked into so every issue should either be closed or tagged If you have an issue that there was no answer and no response then hanging there a few months it would make people feel the project had been abandoned And when closing issues it's important to let the user know why the issue is closed maybe because the issue was a question or maybe we have a duplicated issue when closing issues you have to at least tell why you closed it, otherwise people would be mad about it and another thing to give contributors more permission If a contributor can have permission to close the issue they may be more involved into the project they will think they are part of the project Apart from issues another part of managing the project is to respect the pull requests Pull requests are the passage of the new generation It represents how people like to contribute to your project When people create the project to When people create the pull request to your project they will expect you can review their contribution in time So my suggestion is to When there is a new pull request, you should review them as soon as you can so people can feel valued Not everyone knows how your project works so for most pull requests you may have when that happens you should get them to code review by commenting the problem in the code review People can know your project better and sometimes you can When people don't understand your project code review is a very good way to tell people the details But it doesn't mean we need to merge every pull request it will harm the project because code quality When there are very bad contributions don't hesitate to reject the pull request And if a pull request is not very good but still has value I suggest doing refactoring of the code after merging The last thing to keep the project well-maintained is to make contribution easier There are many ways to achieve that One of the common things is to have a "bootstrap" script which can automatically set up everything your project needs Like setting up the building environment Like synching your submodules It's also important to have detailed documentation on the development of the project itself You can have documentation explaining the architecture of your project and best practice for things Apart from that, you can also tag tasks in the issues for beginners so if someone is interested in contributing to your project but has no idea what to do they can always search for the easy issues and work on that first thing And when the project keeps growing at a certain stage there will be companies starting to use your project For Electron we have Facebook Microsoft, Slack, and various startups building there products based on Electron And that's really a very good thing Apart from passion, money is the only thing that can keep open-souce projects alive But people are doing business based on the project They will put resources on it and the project will always And having company users can attract more users because when a big company is using the project people would be very confident the project can be good and used for a long time and the company's users usually contribute a lot back because they always have their unique requests they have lots of developers and usually they are very good developers and when they need something they would choose to build the things they want and then contribute it back But still, sometimes company users can actually be bad For some companies they're like your busiest users and when they have something that needs to be done you would put those things on the top of your todo list and sometimes that can disturb your original development plan And for some users when they found they need to a new feature of the project or they want to refactor the project for another sometimes they just fork your project instead of contributing things back because most can take a lot of time and for some companies they just decide to fork the project and forking can be very bad for open-source So Electron keeps growing at a certain stage it became a huge task for one developer to maintain These are the emails I got everyday So it's impossible for just me to answer all of the issues and sometimes the new pull requests everyday and I need to take lots of time reviewing them And if I put too much energy into the community I would have no time for developing the project itself which can be very bad Currently Electron became a team so now it's not only me working on the project but have a small team working on Electron Some of the team members are from other teams of GitHub and we also had a few new developers and most importantly we are still hiring So at last if you are interested in joining the Electron team you can send email to this email address groundwater@github.com Groundwater is our new manager of the Electron team If you are interested in Electron just follow him Thank you
